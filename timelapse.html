Timelapses

Sometimes the world moves too fast, and sometimes you can make it move even faster in your DSLR film. Time-lapse photography allows you to control the universe within your movie by capturing a situation in a fraction of the frames, and then playing it back at normal speed making it appear as though life is zipping by. The main focus here is time. A blooming flower, traffic moving on a busy intersection, or people filling a stadium all takes a devotion of time to uniquely depict. In the end, putting in the work helps you transform minutes, hours, or days into a matter of seconds.

First, Have the Right Equipment

To make it time well spent, you’ll need adequate preparation and the right equipment.
What you'll need:

  • Any DSLR camera
  • Digital timer/Intervalometer
  • Sturdy tripod
  • Fresh batteries to last the shoot

Determine your subject matter

You’ll need to figure what you want to capture and how long it will take to show the proper activity. For example, a flower blooming can take several weeks, whereas people embarking or disembarking public transportation may last for only a few minutes.

Place Your Camera on the Tripod

There’s nothing worse than having movement during capture because you didn’t tighten the legs or head on your tripod. It also helps if the tripod you're using has a hook underneath the mount. This can be used for weighing down your tripod to prevent any shake from wind blowing in the area.

Set Your Camera to a Narrow Aperture

With a camera on a tripod and hours of recording ahead of you, you may as well use an aperture setting fit to capturing a maximum depth of field. Aperture refers to your lens opening size. This is because you want to make sure that you get everything in your frame in focus in your timelapse. It is important to remember that aperture size is inversely proportional to the f-stop number. So a small aperture means a large f-stop number. The smallest aperture will depend on your lens, which can range from f/16 to f/22.

Shoot in Manual Mode

If the amount of light in the duration of your shot will stay the same, then manual mode is best. However, if you worry light values will shift too much, try the aperture priority setting. These work because we’re not using the movie mode. It maintains a consistent aperture by adjusting shutter speed when light values change.

Pre-timelapse Calculations

So, before you start making a time-lapse you need to do some calculations. The things needed to be calculated are the shooting interval, clip length, event duration, fps of finished video and the storage size that the pictures will use.

  • The shooting interval refers to the pause after which a new picture is taken. This can vary from situation to situation. For example, if you are shooting moving traffic, fast moving clouds (faster than usual) or a time-lapse of your trip to somewhere from inside your car, the interval you would want is 1 second. If you are shooting sunsets/sunrises, slow crowds or clouds at normal speed you would want an interval of about 1-3 seconds.

  • Clip length refers to the length of the final video. This should be a few minutes at most because watching a time-lapse longer than that tends to be boring.

  • Event duration means how long the event you are time-lapsing is going to last.

  • FPS means 'Frames per Second'. This means how many pictures of your time-lapse there will be in one second of the finished video. This is usually 24 or 30. If you end up with too many pictures you can always make the fps higher to shorten the final video according to your needs. This can be adjusted according to your needs but keep in mind that if you go below 24 fps, the final video will be choppy and unpleasant to watch.

  • Storage size means the size that all of the photos of the time-lapse will take on the camera. This can sometimes be a problem because if you are shooting at a high resolution, the pictures can be of a large size and storing these on your mobile or camera can be a problem. If there is not sufficient space to store the images, the time for which you will be shooting can be decreased, the megapixel count can be decreased or the shooting interval can be increased.

Set your Intervalometer

Once you have calculated the amount of time that you want to capture you entire timelapse and the intervals between each shot, connect your intervalometer to your DSLR and set it to the calculated times.

Start the capturing of your Timelapse

Now, with everything set and when you see that the time is right, hit the shutter button or the intervalometer button to start capturing your timelapse. After your DSLR has finished taking the timelapse, you can use some post-processing software to turn all your shots into a video file.
